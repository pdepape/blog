A five-signal documentation health audit

TechDocs Studio translated a broad documentation-health discussion into five observable operating signals and a lightweight checklist that teams can use as a shared starting point.

First-party implementation by TechDocs Studio

Documentation audits can become long inventories before a team agrees on the problem it is trying to solve. The studio wanted a smaller entry point: enough structure to start a useful conversation about operations without presenting a five-item checklist as a complete assessment.

The challenge

Documentation quality crosses ownership, findability, technical accuracy, launch coordination, and feedback. A homepage tool had to make those dimensions concrete in a few minutes, without asking visitors to submit product information or treating a simple score as a diagnosis.

Constraints

  • The interaction had to work within a static site with no account or backend.
  • Every item needed a plain-language statement that a product team could verify.
  • The checklist had to be keyboard-accessible and usable on narrow screens.
  • No answers could be stored, transmitted, or presented as a formal benchmark.

The approach

The audit uses one observable signal from each of five operating areas:

  1. Ownership: key product areas have an owner and review cadence.
  2. Discovery: important search terms map to clear documentation pages.
  3. Accuracy: API examples are tested against current product behavior.
  4. Launch alignment: release notes connect readers to affected guidance.
  5. Feedback: support tickets feed a recurring documentation backlog.

Each checked item contributes equally to a visible readiness percentage. Equal weighting keeps the interaction understandable, but it is a design simplification rather than a claim that all five signals carry the same risk for every product.

What was built

The finished component combines native checkbox labels, a numeric score, and a progress bar. A small shared script recalculates the percentage in the browser whenever an answer changes. The answers remain on the visitor’s device for the current page session and are not sent to TechDocs Studio.

The surrounding copy positions the result as a fast readiness signal. It invites a team to ask where deeper review is needed rather than implying that the percentage certifies documentation quality.

What it demonstrates

The audit demonstrates how a documentation strategy can be reduced to a focused interactive entry point without hiding its limitations. It does not demonstrate improved support volume, product adoption, or documentation accuracy; those outcomes would require baseline data and a longer engagement.

What to measure next

  • Which signals teams most often identify as missing.
  • Whether the checklist leads to a deeper audit or a defined improvement backlog.
  • Changes in search dead ends, stale examples, and documentation-related support tickets.
  • Completion of ownership and review-cadence actions after an audit.
  • Whether different product types require different signal weights or additional checks.
